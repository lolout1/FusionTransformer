\begin{abstract}
Wearable fall-detection systems must operate under strict resource and latency constraints while maintaining high sensitivity to fall events and robustness to diverse Activity of Daily Life (ADLs). In our setting, a smartwatch collects inertial data (which can be noisy) and performs on-device inference using a lightweight deep learning architecture. The conventional wisdom is to only use a single stream architecture for fall detection on the edge device because it reduces model complexity and inference time. However, single-stream accelerometer data lacks contextual information, such as directional information of motion. In this paper, we explore single-stream (accelerometer only) versus dual-stream (accelerometer data and gyroscope as two separate inputs) architecture and demonstrate that dual stream with Kalman fusion can reduce noise in inertia sensor data and retain the directional information and can achieve a fall detection model that surpasses our previous single-stream models.

\section{Introduction}
A wearable fall detection system is a critical aspect of health monitoring, particularly
for older adults who are at a higher risk of falls [18]. Early fall detection systems relied on specialized hardware put on the chest or waist of the body to monitor sudden changes in movement and body orientation [ 13 ]. These early devices were often bulky and
restricted the free movement of users, leading to low acceptance among users. The increasing prevalence of smartphones and
smartwatches, equipped with multiple inertia sensors and robust computational capabilities, presents an opportunity for the deployment of a fall detection system on low-cost smartwatch 
devices. These devices, already widely adopted by older adults, offer a non-intrusive solution for continuous health monitoring.
Recently, more and more wearable fall detection systems have been implemented using various machine learning algorithms. However,
the source of data used for training the algorithms has predominantly been the
single stream accelerometer data sensed from an edge device due to privacy concerns with visual data. A Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), a
variant of the Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) using an accelerometer
data captured through a wrist-worn watch has been shown to exhibit
better performance than traditional machine learning methods (SVM, Random Forest) in
fall detection after extensive experiments with three different open-
source simulated fall datasets, as well as real-world tests involving
five young adults of varying heights and weights [11].
Transformer models have risen in popularity in recent years. Their
success in the Natural Language Processing (NLP) task has made
them a viable architecture for fall detection because  of their ability

Despite all the advancements, current fall detection systems deployed on wearable devices 
struggle with accurately distinguishing the Activities of Daily Living
(ADLs) from soft falls, which are common among older adults. Most
existing models rely heavily on accelerometer data 
which limits the contextual information, such as orientation information, that can be obtained from the gyroscope. However, our prior work in~\cite{awatif} and our recent investigation (November 2025) identified a critical challenge: accelerometer-only models outperformed accelerometer+gyroscope models (87.35\% vs 85.30\% using the F1 score metric) when trained with the same dataset.   This motivates us to investigate why gyroscopic data, which seems to provide more contextual information, is not helpful. Our investigation leads to a dual stream architecture for accelerometer and gyroscope data as two separate stream inputs optimized with a Kalman fusion and temporal attention pooling. We demonstrated via various ablation studies that our dual stream architecture with Kalman Fusion and channel-aware normalization can achieve the highest F1 score of 91.10\% for a wearable fall detection system.
