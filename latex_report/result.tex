\section{Results}
\label{sec:Results}

We present comprehensive experimental results evaluating the impact of Kalman fusion on fall detection performance. All experiments use 21-fold Leave-One-Subject-Out Cross-Validation (LOSO-CV) with young subjects as test folds and elderly subjects contributing ADL samples for training. We report F1 score as the primary metric given the class imbalance inherent in fall detection (falls are rare events). Each experiment was conducted with a fixed random seed for reproducibility.

\subsubsection{Why Kalman Fusion Helps Transformers}

Kalman fusion transforms raw gyroscope angular velocity ($\omega_x, \omega_y, \omega_z$ in rad/s) into orientation angles (roll, pitch, yaw in radians) by fusing accelerometer and gyroscope measurements. The transformer's self-attention mechanism computes global temporal dependencies through:
\begin{equation}
\text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V
\end{equation}
Raw angular velocity is difficult to interpret---the same $\omega_z$ value could mean standing still with slight drift or mid-fall rotation. Kalman-fused orientation provides \textit{absolute} body pose information: a roll angle of $-\pi/2$ unambiguously indicates a side-lying position regardless of how fast the rotation occurred. This semantic clarity enables transformers to learn meaningful temporal attention patterns over body orientation changes during falls.

\subsubsection{Why Kalman Fusion Hurts CNNs}

CNN convolutions excel at detecting local patterns through learned kernels. Fall detection with CNNs relies heavily on detecting \textit{magnitude spikes}---sudden high-amplitude changes in angular velocity that accompany impact. The raw gyroscope captures these transient events directly. However, Kalman fusion converts velocity to position (orientation), transforming sharp spikes into gradual angle changes. A fall that produces a distinctive 500$^\circ$/s spike in raw gyroscope becomes a smooth 90$^\circ$ orientation change after fusion. The CNN kernels, optimized for detecting local discontinuities, find less distinctive patterns in the fused representation.

\subsubsection{Per-Subject Kalman Effect Variability}

While Kalman fusion improves the \textit{average} F1 score by +1.40\%, a detailed per-subject analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:per_subject_kalman}) reveals considerable variance across individuals. Of the 21 test subjects:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{10 subjects improved} with Kalman fusion (47.6\%)
    \item \textbf{9 subjects degraded} with Kalman fusion (42.9\%)
    \item \textbf{2 subjects unchanged} ($<$1\% delta, 9.5\%)
\end{itemize}

The subjects with the largest improvements (S36: +23.6\%, S44: +10.1\%) had poor raw-input performance (58.4\% and 64.4\% F1 respectively), suggesting Kalman fusion is particularly beneficial when raw signals are noisy or ambiguous. Conversely, subjects with the largest degradations (S34: -6.0\%, S31: -5.7\%) had strong raw-input performance ($>$91\% F1), indicating Kalman filtering may remove discriminative high-frequency information in these cases.

This variability suggests potential for \textit{adaptive} fusion strategies where Kalman filtering is applied selectively based on signal quality metrics---an avenue for future work.

\subsection{Channel-Aware Normalization}
\label{sec:channel_aware_norm}

We discovered that the standard preprocessing approach of normalizing all Kalman output channels together is suboptimal. The Kalman filter produces a 7-channel output: accelerometer features [SMV, $a_x$, $a_y$, $a_z$] and orientation angles [roll, pitch, yaw]. These have fundamentally different characteristics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accelerometer}: Unbounded during falls (spikes up to 30+ m/s$^2$), requires normalization for stable training
    \item \textbf{Orientation}: Bounded in $\pm\pi$ radians with physical meaning (roll=0 means level, pitch=$\pi/2$ means vertical)
\end{itemize}

Normalizing all channels together using StandardScaler mixes these different distributions, potentially destroying the physical interpretation of orientation angles.

The ``separate'' normalization approach (normalizing accelerometer and orientation independently) performs worse than both alternatives, suggesting that preserving the absolute scale of orientation angles is more important than having both modalities on similar scales.

Signal analysis of struggling subjects revealed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lower impact forces}: Struggling subjects exhibit 20\% lower Signal Magnitude Vector (SMV) during falls, indicating gentler impacts that are harder to distinguish from ADLs.
    \item \textbf{Similar ADL patterns}: The low-impact falls in these subjects closely resemble sitting or lying down activities, increasing misclassification risk.
    \item \textbf{Orientation ambiguity}: Kalman-fused orientation changes are smaller for gentle falls, reducing the discriminative power of orientation features.
\end{itemize}

These findings suggest that future work should focus on: (1) data augmentation to simulate gentle falls, (2) subject-specific calibration, or (3) multi-modal fusion incorporating additional context (e.g., activity history).

\subsection{Summary of Key Findings}

Our experimental evaluation leads to the following conclusions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Kalman fusion improves transformer-based fall detection}: Under controlled conditions with identical architecture, Kalman-fused orientation features with channel-aware normalization outperform raw gyroscope data by +1.61\% F1 score (91.10\% vs 89.49\%) for transformers. However, the effect is variable across subjects: 10/21 subjects improved while 9/21 degraded, with the largest gains for subjects with poor raw-input performance.

    \item \textbf{SE and TAP synergy with Kalman fusion}: The combination of SE attention and TAP pooling provides the greatest benefit when used with Kalman fusion (+2.02\% over raw with SE+TAP: 91.10\% vs 89.08\%).

    \item \textbf{Dual-stream architecture is superior}: The dual-stream transformer (86.99\% $\rightarrow$ 91.10\%) significantly outperforms single-stream alternatives (+4.11\%), validating separate processing of accelerometer and orientation streams.

    \item \textbf{Architecture-specific input preference}: While transformers benefit from Kalman fusion, CNN-Mamba performs better with raw input (89.98\% vs 88.09\%), suggesting CNNs are better suited for high-frequency raw signal processing.

    \item \textbf{Focal loss benefits imbalanced classification}: Focal loss improves recall by +6.41\% compared to BCE, which is critical for fall detection where missing falls has severe consequences.

    \item \textbf{Channel-aware normalization provides further improvement}: Normalizing only accelerometer channels while keeping Kalman orientation in raw radians improves F1 by +0.23\% (90.87\%$\rightarrow$91.10\%), preserving the physical meaning of orientation angles.

    \item \textbf{Best configuration achieves 91.10\% F1}: The optimal configuration combines Kalman fusion, dual-stream transformer architecture with SE attention and temporal attention pooling, channel-aware normalization (acc-only), 50:50 embedding ratio, and focal loss, achieving 91.10\% F1 score on 21-fold LOSO-CV.

    \item \textbf{Transformer outperforms CNN}: Under identical training conditions with Kalman fusion, the dual-stream transformer (91.10\% F1) outperforms the CNN baseline (88.86\% F1) by +2.23\%. The Transformer wins on 14/21 subjects (66.7\%), with the largest gains on challenging subjects exhibiting atypical fall patterns.

    \item \textbf{Failure modes stem from gentle falls}: Subjects with F1 $<$ 80\% exhibit 20\% lower impact forces during falls, making their patterns harder to distinguish from ADLs. This represents a fundamental challenge in fall detection that may require subject-specific calibration or data augmentation targeting gentle falls.
\end{enumerate}
